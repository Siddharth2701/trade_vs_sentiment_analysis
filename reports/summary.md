## Methodology

The analysis combines trade execution data with the Fear & Greed Index to study how market sentiment influences trader decision-making.

First, trade records were aggregated at a daily trader level to capture behavioral patterns rather than individual executions. From this data, key behavioral metrics were created including daily profit/loss, win rate, trading activity (number of executions), leverage usage, long/short bias, and a drawdown proxy measuring psychological pressure from cumulative losses.

The trader behavior dataset was then aligned with daily market sentiment classifications (Extreme Fear to Extreme Greed). To further understand performance differences, traders were segmented into behavioral groups such as high vs low leverage users, frequent vs infrequent traders, and consistent vs inconsistent performers.

The analysis focused on identifying relationships between sentiment conditions and trader actions rather than predicting price movements.

---

## Key Insights

1. **Fear increases trading but reduces performance**
   Traders execute significantly more trades during fear periods, yet their win rate drops. This indicates panic-driven overtrading.

2. **Risk-taking spikes during losses**
   Leverage usage is highest during extreme fear conditions, suggesting revenge trading behavior after losses rather than calculated risk-taking.

3. **Bottom-catching behavior observed**
   Traders show a strong long bias during falling markets, attempting to predict reversals prematurely.

4. **Discipline improves in greedy markets**
   During greed phases, traders trade less frequently and achieve higher win rates, indicating better decision-making when markets trend clearly.

5. **Behavior matters more than market direction**
   Performance differences are driven more by trader psychology than by sentiment itself.

---

## Strategy Recommendations

1. **Reduce activity in extreme fear**
   Limit the number of trades during panic market conditions to avoid emotional decisions.

2. **Control leverage during volatility**
   Apply stricter leverage caps during fear phases to prevent large drawdowns.

3. **Trade with trend, not against it**
   Allow aggressive positions only when market sentiment supports directional movement.

4. **Avoid premature reversal trades**
   Block counter-trend long positions during strong downward sentiment to reduce bottom-catching losses.

---

## Conclusion

The study shows that trader psychology significantly impacts performance. Traders perform worst when reacting emotionally to market stress and best when trading selectively in stable trends. Behavior-based trading rules can therefore improve results without requiring price prediction models.
